The problem

Speed isn't the same as quality

For years, mobile operators competed almost exclusively on peak download speeds — headline figures that were easy to advertise but told consumers almost nothing about their day-to-day experience. A network that could occasionally hit gigabit speeds under ideal conditions, but delivered a poor connection the majority of the time, scored the same as one that consistently worked well for everyone.

These top speeds are vanity metrics. They measure the best a network can theoretically do, not what it actually delivers to ordinary people in the places they live and work. The question they cannot answer is the only one that matters to most users:

"How often does my network just work — well enough to do the things I actually need to do?"

Consistent Quality was created to answer that question, with a fully open and transparent methodology that any operator, regulator, researcher or consumer body can inspect, use and build upon — free of charge.

Before Consistent Quality

Industry ranked networks by peak download speed. Marketing, investment and engineering were all optimised toward achieving occasional top speeds. Consumers had no reliable way to compare networks on what mattered. Regulators had no open standard to hold operators accountable.

With Consistent Quality

Networks are measured — and now compete — on how reliably they deliver a good experience for ordinary users. Operators in dozens of countries bonus staff on Consistent Quality scores. Ofcom uses it to inform consumers. The industry has fundamentally changed what it optimises for.

The methodology

What makes a connection
"good enough"?

Consistent Quality defines "good enough" as a connection that is reliably capable of fulfilling most users' expectations when using any of the five application types that people use most on their mobile devices — at typical settings, not laboratory maximums.

A good network should enable a user to use any of these application types and get a good experience — whenever and wherever they are. Consistent Quality measures how often that is actually true.

How it's calculated

A pass/fail test, run millions of times

The calculation is straightforward and fully transparent. For each test taken on a network:

1

A series of measurements are taken simultaneously — download speed, upload speed, latency, jitter, packet loss, and time to first byte.

2

If all six measurements pass their respective thresholds, the test is a pass. If any single measurement falls below its threshold, the whole test is a fail. A fast download speed cannot compensate for bad latency.

3

The Consistent Quality score is the percentage of tests that passed. Example: 60 passes out of 100 tests = 60% Consistent Quality. Higher is better.

This all-or-nothing approach reflects real user experience: a video call that fails because of high packet loss is a bad experience, regardless of how fast the download speed is.

The six KPIs

Different apps need different things

Not all KPIs matter equally to all applications. The table below shows which KPIs most affect each application type — and why the thresholds are set where they are, grounded in published standards from Netflix, Google, ITU, Cisco, GSMA and others.

KPI What it measures Most affects Core Excellent
Download speed Data received from network 📺 🌐 📱 ≥ 1.5 Mbps ≥ 5 Mbps
Upload speed Data sent to network 📹 📱 ≥ 500 Kbps ≥ 1.5 Mbps
Latency Round-trip response time 📹 🌐 📱 🎮 ≤ 100 ms ≤ 50 ms
Jitter Variation in latency 📹 🎮 ≤ 20 ms ≤ 12 ms
Packet loss Data packets lost in transit 📹 🎮 ≤ 4% ≤ 1%
Time to first byte Time before data starts arriving 📺 🌐 📱 ≤ 1.2 s ≤ 0.8 s

Why these thresholds? Each threshold is grounded in published research and recommendations from major standards bodies and application providers: Netflix (5 Mbps for HD streaming), ITU G.114 (latency for VoIP), Google web.dev (800ms TTFB), Cisco and Microsoft Teams (jitter and packet loss for video calls), 3GPP TS 23.501 (gaming latency), and GSMA network experience research. Thresholds are reviewed periodically as application requirements evolve.

Global adoption

From one report to a global standard

Since its introduction in 2018, Consistent Quality has been adopted by mobile operators, regulators and consumer organisations across more than 40 countries. Operators now use it not only to report network quality publicly, but to set internal targets, drive engineering investment and incentivise staff.

Adopted by Ofcom — the UK communications regulator

In 2025, Ofcom adopted Consistent Quality as the foundation of Map Your Mobile — a public tool that allows UK consumers to check real-world mobile network performance at their home or work postcode, at 50-metre resolution. In June 2026, Ofcom will announce that a derivative of Consistent Quality will be used to measure and report on all UK mobile operators, making it a formal UK regulatory standard.

Open source

Fully open.
No black box.

When Consistent Quality was created in 2018, every other major network measurement company had taken the opposite approach. RootMetrics (RootScore), Umlaut (Umlaut Score) and others protected their methodologies as proprietary commercial assets — available only to paying customers, with the inner workings deliberately withheld from public scrutiny.

Consistent Quality was designed from the outset to be different. The complete methodology — every threshold, every KPI, every calculation — is published openly and is free for anyone to inspect, use, adapt or build upon. No licence fees. No access restrictions. No gatekeeping.

Why openness matters

A methodology that anyone can scrutinise is one that everyone can trust. Operators can adopt it knowing it is independent and unbiased. Regulators can use it knowing its basis is transparent and defensible. Consumers can rely on it knowing it has not been shaped by commercial interests. And researchers can build on it, challenge it, and improve it over time.

This is not how network measurement worked in 2018. Consistent Quality was a deliberate break from industry convention — a choice to put public benefit ahead of commercial advantage.

Origin

How Consistent Quality came to be

Consistent Quality was invented by Tom Luke in 2018, while serving as Vice President at Tutela Technologies — a Canadian mobile network measurement company later acquired by Opensignal.

Tom identified that the mobile industry's reliance on peak speed metrics was failing consumers. He conceived Consistent Quality as a consumer-first alternative, advocated for it to be made fully open-source rather than proprietary, and spent the following seven years working to convince operators, regulators and consumer organisations around the world to adopt it.

Tom received no patent income, no royalties and no financial reward for the invention. His goal throughout has been public benefit: better connectivity for ordinary people, driven by an industry that competes on the right things.

September 2018

Consistent Quality invented and named

Tom Luke conceives the metric at Tutela Technologies, defines the KPIs and thresholds, and proposes the name "Consistent Quality". The methodology is made fully open-source.

October 2018

First public launch

Consistent Quality debuts publicly in Tutela's US Mobile LTE Network Quality 2018 report. Verizon adopts it immediately in customer-facing marketing materials.

2019 – 2020

Global operator adoption begins

Operators across North America, Latin America, Europe and Southeast Asia begin using Consistent Quality in public reporting. TIM Brazil and Verizon begin bonusing staff on the metric.

2020

Opensignal acquires Tutela

Comlinkdata combines Tutela and Opensignal. Consistent Quality becomes a central metric across Opensignal's global reporting platform, expanding its reach significantly.

2022 – 2024

Japan and Italy adopt at scale

All four major Japanese operators are measured. KDDI and SoftBank headline corporate press releases with Consistent Quality wins. TIM Italy begins bonusing its entire technical staff. The incoming President of NTT DoCoMo publicly sets winning Consistent Quality as his company's primary target.

2025

Ofcom adopts Consistent Quality for UK consumers

Ofcom adopts Consistent Quality as the foundation of Map Your Mobile — a public tool giving UK consumers postcode-level mobile network quality data. Tom Luke is quoted at the launch. Which? and GoCompare also adopt the metric for consumer comparison tools.

June 2026

UK regulatory standard confirmed

Ofcom announces that a derivative of Consistent Quality will be used to measure and report on all UK mobile operators — enshrining it as a formal UK regulatory standard for consumer-facing network quality.
